Pension System

The District of Columbia operates its own retirement system. As a federal district, DC's pension obligations have a unique history — the federal government assumed responsibility for pre-1997 pension liabilities through the National Capital Revitalization and Self-Government Improvement Act.

Competing Analytical Perspectives

The same fiscal data can support different policy conclusions depending on the analytical framework applied. We present three major perspectives:

Equity & Adequacy

How does the District's tax structure distribute the burden across income levels? DC faces unique equity challenges as a city-state with significant income inequality, a large federal workforce, and no ability to tax the income of suburban commuters who earn wages in the District.

Efficiency & Growth

DC's revenue base is constrained by the inability to tax commuter income and the presence of tax-exempt federal property covering roughly 42% of the District's land area. How do these structural limitations affect service delivery and competitiveness for residents and businesses?

Process & Governance

The District operates under congressional oversight, with Congress retaining authority to review and override DC legislation, including its budget. This creates unique governance challenges not faced by any state. DC publishes an annual ACFR and maintains its own financial reporting standards.
